Before my twenties, I thought learning worked like this: I take in a concept, it
locks into my mind, and when it shows up on an exam I know how to answer the
question. As I went through college and graduate school, though, that model
stopped holding up. A different pattern took its place. I learn something new, I
notice how it connects to ideas I already know, and those connections build into
a graph — where each node is a concept and each edge is the relationship between
them.

But this graph isn't always stable. Sometimes you lose an edge, and with it your
way back to the node. Other times you just need a small hint to re-ignite the
connection. That's where Vannevar Bush's *As We May Think* comes in.

### 1945's Idea

Writing in 1945, Bush imagined a device he called the **memex**: a desk where a
person could store all their books, notes, and records, and call any of them up
instantly. What made it special wasn't the storage — it was how things were
linked. Instead of filing each item under a single category, you could tie
records together by association, building **trails** that ran from one idea to
the next. To return to a thought, you didn't have to remember where you filed
it; you just followed the trail. Bush's bet was that this mirrors how the mind
actually works — by association rather than rigid indexes.

That's exactly the experience I'd been describing to myself as a graph. The
trails Bush wanted to capture are my edges, and the records are my nodes. He saw,
decades before any of the tools existed, that the hard part of knowledge isn't
holding facts — it's preserving the connections between them, and being able to
find your way back when an edge starts to fade. That's the problem I want memex
to solve.

### Dejaview - A piece born too early

If Bush sketched the idea, a group at Columbia tried to build it. I came across
it almost by accident. Searching Hacker News for products similar to what I had
in mind, the very first post I opened had one of the authors in the comments,
linking the paper himself. In 2007, Oren Laadan, Jason Nieh, and their
colleagues published **DejaView**, a "personal virtual computer recorder." It continuously recorded everything that happened on
a desktop — the visual output, the application and file system state, and the
text shown on screen — and indexed it so you could later browse, search, and
even time-travel back to any earlier moment and pick up where you left off. It
was, in a very literal sense, a working memex: a complete, searchable record of
your computing life.

What strikes me is that it arrived almost a decade too early. DejaView could
capture the *nodes* — every screen, every document, the full timeline of what
you did — but it had no good way to recover the *edges*. In 2007 the only handle
on all that data was keyword search. There were no large language models to read
the captured text, understand what you were doing, and reconstruct the
associations between moments. The recording was rich; the leverage wasn't there
yet. The hard part — turning a pile of captured records back into connected,
retrievable thought — was exactly the piece the technology of the time couldn't
deliver. That gap is what makes building memex feel possible now.

### RAG - my POC

So I started with the piece that's actually tractable today: recovering the
edges. My first proof of concept is a personal knowledge engine built around
retrieval-augmented generation. You feed it documents — PDFs, Markdown, plain
text — and it parses them, breaks them into sections, and embeds each one into a
vector space, so that meaning, not just keywords, becomes searchable. Then you
ask a question in plain language and it pulls back the passages that actually
relate, and an LLM answers on top of them.

In the node-and-edge terms from earlier: the documents are the nodes, and the
embeddings are a way to rediscover the edges on demand. Instead of remembering
where I filed something, I describe what I'm reaching for and let semantic
similarity reconstruct the connection. This is the part DejaView couldn't do in
2007 — and the part that makes the whole idea feel buildable now.

### Current Status

The web app is the most complete piece. Today it can ingest PDF, Markdown, and
text files; PDFs go through a layout-aware parser so tables and figures survive.
Retrieval is hybrid — vector similarity and keyword search fused together and
re-ranked — and you can pick between several OpenAI and Anthropic models for the
ingestion step. It's a Next.js app backed by Postgres with `pgvector`, and it
also exposes an MCP server so other tools (like Claude Desktop) can query the
knowledge base directly.

The frontier I'm working toward is the DejaView half: bringing the right memory
to you instead of making you go search for it. I have a desktop client (built
with Tauri) that already does a first version of this. It watches your text
selection anywhere on the system — highlight a sentence in any app — and pops up
a small heads-up overlay showing related things from your knowledge base, right
next to your cursor. Today that lookup runs against the cloud retrieval engine;
moving the embedding and the index fully on-device, and expanding from
"selection" to passive background capture, is the part still under construction.

### Challenges known

The honest part. The biggest issues so far are about **fidelity** — making sure
what gets indexed actually matches what you put in:

- **Chunking.** Splitting documents by meaning works well for varied text but
  misfires on dense, single-topic material and on very short documents, where it
  produces oversized or awkward sections.
- **Summarization drift.** When an LLM helps structure the text, it sometimes
  paraphrases or even translates instead of reproducing the source faithfully —
  quietly degrading what you can later retrieve. I'm leaning toward an
  extraction-only approach for plain text to remove that risk.

And the harder problem still ahead is the capture side: doing always-on,
system-wide recording responsibly — which means real privacy controls and the
user staying in charge of what's kept. That's the part that has to be right
before any of the rest matters.

### Where this is going

The end goal is bigger than search over a few uploaded files. I want memex to
record every **episode** of what you do on your computer — the documents you
read, the things you wrote, the pages and apps you moved through — and treat
those episodes as first-class memory alongside the files you deliberately save.

With both halves in one place — the documents you chose to keep and the
episodes of how you actually worked — the system can do the thing a plain search
box never could: not just hand back a file, but reconstruct a moment. Remind you
*what* you were doing when you last touched an idea, *what* it connected to, and
*why* it mattered — enough of a hint to re-ignite the memory and pick the trail
back up.

That's the whole bet. Storage was solved decades ago. The part still worth
building is the part that helps you remember.
